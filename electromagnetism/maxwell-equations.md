# Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell's equations unify electricity and magnetism into a single framework. They describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated by charges and currents, and how they interact with each other. This document uses [vector calculus](../math-foundations/vector-calculus.md) extensively. The [covariant formulation](maxwell-covariant.md) reveals their relativistic structure.

## The Four Equations

In differential form (SI units):

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} &= \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} & \text{(Gauss's law)} \\[8pt]
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= 0 & \text{(No magnetic monopoles)} \\[8pt]
\nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} & \text{(Faraday's law)} \\[8pt]
\nabla \times \mathbf{B} &= \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} & \text{(Ampère-Maxwell law)}
\end{aligned}
}
$$

**What this means:** These four equations completely determine the behavior of electromagnetic fields. Given the charge density $\rho$ and current density $\mathbf{J}$, they specify the electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ everywhere.

## Physical Meaning of Each Equation

### Gauss's Law for Electricity

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}
$$

**Integral form:**
$$
\oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\epsilon_0}
$$

**What this means:** Electric field lines originate from positive charges and terminate on negative charges. The total flux through any closed surface equals the enclosed charge divided by $\epsilon_0$.

**Application:** This gives Coulomb's law for point charges and determines electric fields with high symmetry. See [electrostatics](electrostatics.md).

### Gauss's Law for Magnetism

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0
$$

**Integral form:**
$$
\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0
$$

**What this means:** There are no magnetic monopoles—magnetic field lines always form closed loops. Every magnetic "north" comes with a "south."

**Consequence:** The magnetic field can be written as the curl of a vector potential: $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$.

### Faraday's Law

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}
$$

**Integral form:**
$$
\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}
$$

where $\Phi_B = \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$ is the magnetic flux.

**What this means:** A changing magnetic field induces an electric field. This is the principle behind generators, transformers, and inductors.

**Lenz's Law:** The minus sign means the induced field opposes the change in flux—nature resists changes in magnetic flux.

### Ampère-Maxwell Law

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}
$$

**Integral form:**
$$
\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}
$$

**What this means:** Magnetic fields are generated by currents (Ampère's original law) AND by changing electric fields (Maxwell's displacement current).

**Maxwell's insight:** The displacement current term $\epsilon_0 \partial \mathbf{E}/\partial t$ was Maxwell's crucial addition. Without it, charge conservation would be violated. With it, electromagnetic waves become possible.

## The Constants

| Constant | Symbol | SI Value | Meaning |
|----------|--------|----------|---------|
| Permittivity of free space | $\epsilon_0$ | $8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ F/m | How easily electric fields form in vacuum |
| Permeability of free space | $\mu_0$ | $4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ H/m | How easily magnetic fields form in vacuum |
| Speed of light | $c$ | $3 \times 10^8$ m/s | $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}$ |

**What this means:** Maxwell discovered that $1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}$ equals the speed of light—strong evidence that light is an electromagnetic wave. See [electromagnetic waves](electromagnetic-waves.md).

## Integral Forms Summary

| Law | Integral Form |
|-----|---------------|
| Gauss (E) | $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = Q_{\text{enc}}/\epsilon_0$ |
| Gauss (B) | $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$ |
| Faraday | $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ |
| Ampère-Maxwell | $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0(I_{\text{enc}} + \epsilon_0 d\Phi_E/dt)$ |

## Charge Conservation

Taking the divergence of the Ampère-Maxwell law and using $\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) = 0$:

$$
0 = \mu_0 \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E})
$$

Using Gauss's law $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0$:

$$
\boxed{\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0}
$$

**What this means:** This is the continuity equation—charge is conserved. The rate of change of charge density equals the negative divergence of current (charge flowing out).

## Potentials

Since $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$, we can write:
$$
\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}
$$

Faraday's law then gives:
$$
\nabla \times \left(\mathbf{E} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}}{\partial t}\right) = 0
$$

So we can write:
$$
\mathbf{E} = -\nabla \phi - \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}}{\partial t}
$$

**What this means:** The fields can be derived from a scalar potential $\phi$ and vector potential $\mathbf{A}$. This reduces six field components to four potential components.

### Gauge Freedom

The potentials are not unique. The **gauge transformation**:
$$
\phi \to \phi - \frac{\partial \chi}{\partial t}, \qquad \mathbf{A} \to \mathbf{A} + \nabla \chi
$$

leaves $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ unchanged for any function $\chi$.

**Common gauges:**
- **Coulomb gauge:** $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0$
- **Lorenz gauge:** $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = 0$

## Maxwell's Equations in Matter

In materials, we define:
- **Electric displacement:** $\mathbf{D} = \epsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P}$ (where $\mathbf{P}$ is polarization)
- **Magnetic field strength:** $\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{B}/\mu_0 - \mathbf{M}$ (where $\mathbf{M}$ is magnetization)

Maxwell's equations become:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} &= \rho_f \\
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= 0 \\
\nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \\
\nabla \times \mathbf{H} &= \mathbf{J}_f + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}
\end{aligned}
$$

where $\rho_f$ and $\mathbf{J}_f$ are free (not bound) charges and currents.

**For linear materials:**
$$
\mathbf{D} = \epsilon \mathbf{E}, \qquad \mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H}
$$

## Energy and Momentum

### Energy Density

$$
u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2 + \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2
$$

### Poynting Vector

The energy flux (power per unit area) is:

$$
\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B}
$$

### Energy Conservation

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{S} = -\mathbf{J} \cdot \mathbf{E}
$$

**What this means:** The rate of change of electromagnetic energy plus the energy flowing out equals the work done on charges (which can be negative if charges do work on fields).

### Momentum Density

$$
\mathbf{g} = \epsilon_0 (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B}) = \frac{\mathbf{S}}{c^2}
$$

**What this means:** Electromagnetic fields carry momentum. This is how light exerts radiation pressure.

## Boundary Conditions

At an interface between two media:

| Condition | Equation |
|-----------|----------|
| Normal D | $D_{1\perp} - D_{2\perp} = \sigma_f$ (surface charge) |
| Normal B | $B_{1\perp} = B_{2\perp}$ |
| Tangential E | $E_{1\parallel} = E_{2\parallel}$ |
| Tangential H | $H_{1\parallel} - H_{2\parallel} = K_f$ (surface current) |

## Connection to Special Relativity

Maxwell's equations are inherently relativistic—they're invariant under Lorentz transformations, not Galilean. This was the clue that led Einstein to [special relativity](../relativity/special-relativity.md).

In the [covariant formulation](maxwell-covariant.md), all four equations reduce to:

$$
\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu, \qquad \partial_{[\lambda} F_{\mu\nu]} = 0
$$

where $F^{\mu\nu}$ is the electromagnetic field tensor.

## Summary

| Equation | Differential | What It Describes |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Gauss (E) | $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0$ | Charges source electric fields |
| Gauss (B) | $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ | No magnetic monopoles |
| Faraday | $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t$ | Changing B induces E |
| Ampère-Maxwell | $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0 \partial \mathbf{E}/\partial t$ | Currents and changing E create B |

**The essential insight:** Maxwell's equations unify electricity and magnetism, predict electromagnetic waves traveling at speed $c$, and are inherently relativistic. They represent one of the greatest achievements in physics—showing that light, radio waves, X-rays, and gamma rays are all the same phenomenon at different frequencies.
